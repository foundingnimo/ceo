## Part 1: The Four Components of EQ

Emotional intelligence isn't one skill. It's four:

| Component | Definition | CEO Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Self-Awareness** | Knowing your emotions, triggers, and biases | Prevents reactive decisions |
| **Self-Regulation** | Managing your reactions under pressure | Maintains team stability |
| **Empathy** | Understanding others' perspectives and emotions | Builds trust and loyalty |
| **Social Skills** | Managing relationships and influence | Enables effective leadership |

Most CEOs overestimate their EQ. Studies show leaders consistently rate themselves higher than their teams rate them.

## Part 2: Self-Awareness â€” Knowing Your Triggers

Self-awareness is the foundation. You can't manage what you don't recognize.

### Common CEO Triggers

| Trigger | Typical Reaction | Better Response |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Feeling challenged or questioned | Defensive, dismissive | Curious: "Tell me more about your concern" |
| Missing targets | Searching for blame | Ownership: "What can we learn?" |
| Surprises | Anger, frustration | Pause: "I need a moment to process" |
| Perceived incompetence | Micromanaging | Coaching: "What support do you need?" |
| Time pressure | Curtness, rushing | Acknowledgment: "I'm stressed. Let me reset." |

### Identifying Your Triggers

Ask yourself:
1. When was the last time I reacted in a way I later regretted?
2. What was happening right before that reaction?
3. What pattern do I see across multiple incidents?

Ask others:
- "When do I seem most stressed or reactive?"
- "What triggers should I be more aware of?"

### Understanding Your Biases

Everyone has cognitive biases. CEOs are especially prone to:

| Bias | What It Is | CEO Manifestation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Confirmation bias | Seeking info that confirms beliefs | Ignoring bad news about your strategy |
| Sunk cost fallacy | Continuing because you've invested | Sticking with failing initiatives |
| Attribution error | Blaming others, crediting yourself | "I succeeded; they failed" |
| Overconfidence | Overestimating your judgment | Ignoring expert advice |

## Part 3: Self-Regulation â€” Managing Under Pressure

Knowing your triggers isn't enough. You need techniques to respond rather than react.

### The STOP Technique

When you feel triggered:

| Step | Action | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **S**top | Pause before responding | Interrupt the automatic reaction |
| **T**ake a breath | Slow, deep breath | Activates parasympathetic nervous system |
| **O**bserve | Notice what you're feeling | Create distance from the emotion |
| **P**roceed | Choose your response intentionally | Respond, don't react |

### Recovery Phrases

Have phrases ready for when you need time:
- "That's an interesting point. Let me think about it."
- "I want to give this the attention it deserves. Can we revisit tomorrow?"
- "I'm noticing I'm having a strong reaction. Give me a moment."

### The 10-Minute Rule

For important decisions or responses when you're emotional:
- Write the email/response, then wait 10 minutes before sending
- The bigger the stakes, the longer the wait

## Part 4: Empathy â€” Understanding Others

Empathy isn't sympathy (feeling sorry for someone) or agreement (they're right). It's understanding their perspective.

### Three Types of Empathy

| Type | What It Is | CEO Application |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cognitive** | Understanding how someone thinks | Anticipating board concerns |
| **Emotional** | Feeling what someone feels | Supporting struggling team member |
| **Compassionate** | Understanding + motivation to help | Taking action on what you understand |

### Building Empathy Practices

| Practice | How to Do It |
| :--- | :--- |
| Active listening | Listen to understand, not to respond |
| Perspective-taking | Before reacting, ask "What might they be experiencing?" |
| Skip-level conversations | Talk to people 2-3 levels down regularly |
| Shadow a role | Spend a day doing someone else's job |
| Ask follow-up questions | "How did that make you feel?" |

## Part 5: Seeking and Receiving Feedback

The CEO role is often isolated. Actively seeking feedback is essential for continued growth.

### Why CEOs Get Less Feedback

| Reason | Reality |
| :--- | :--- |
| Power dynamics | People fear negative consequences |
| Success narrative | "You're the CEO, you must know" |
| Filtered information | Reports tell you what you want to hear |
| Ego protection | You may unconsciously discourage it |

### How to Get Honest Feedback

| Technique | How |
| :--- | :--- |
| Ask specific questions | "What's one thing I could do better in 1:1s?" not "Any feedback?" |
| Normalize criticism | "I'm working on Xâ€”I'd appreciate honest input when you see me struggling" |
| Respond well publicly | Thank people who give hard feedback; others will notice |
| Use anonymous surveys | 360 reviews, engagement surveys |
| Find a truth-teller | One person who will always be candid |

### Receiving Feedback Well

| Do | Don't |
| :--- | :--- |
| Thank them | Defend or explain |
| Ask clarifying questions | Dismiss or minimize |
| Reflect before responding | React immediately |
| Follow up on what you've changed | Let it disappear |

## CEO Action Item: Identify Your Top 3 Triggers

Take 15 minutes to build self-awareness:

1. **Reflect on the past month:** When did you react in ways you later regretted?
2. **Identify 3 triggers:** What situations or behaviors set you off?
3. **For each trigger, write:**
   - What typically triggers me: [situation]
   - My typical reaction: [behavior]
   - A better response would be: [alternative]
4. **Choose one practice** to implement this week (STOP technique, 10-minute rule, recovery phrases)
5. **Ask one trusted person:** "What triggers should I be aware of that I might not see?"

**Remember:** Self-awareness is a practice, not a destination. Check in with yourself regularly.
