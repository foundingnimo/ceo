## Part 1: What is Psychological Safety?

**Psychological Safety** is the belief that you won't be punished or humiliated for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes.

It's *not* about being nice. It's not about avoiding hard conversations. It's about creating an environment where **truth travels fast**â€”where a junior engineer can tell you the launch plan is broken before you find out from a customer.

## Part 3: How CEOs Kill (or Build) Safety

**Safety-Killing Behaviors:**
- Reacting poorly to bad news ("Why didn't you catch this earlier?")
- Making decisions then asking for input
- Praising "heroes" who save the day (encourages hiding problems until they're emergencies)
- Punishing mistakes rather than learning from them

**Safety-Building Behaviors:**
- Thanking people for raising concerns, even when wrong
- Saying "I don't know" and "I was wrong"
- Publicly framing failures as learning opportunities
- Asking "What am I missing?" in every meeting

## Part 4: Empowerment vs. Delegation

**Delegation:** "Do this task exactly as I've described."
**Empowerment:** "Here's the outcome we need. You figure out how to get there."

Empowerment requires:
1. **Clear outcomes** (not tasks)
2. **Defined boundaries** (what you can *not* do)
3. **Resources and support**
4. **Permission to fail** (as long as you learn)

## CEO Action Item: The Speak-Up Audit

In your next leadership team meeting, ask:

1. "What's one thing we're doing that isn't working, that no one wants to talk about?"
2. Wait. Sit in the silence. Don't fill it.
3. When someone speaks, thank them for raising it. **Do not defend or explain.** Just listen.
4. End with: "What will it take to fix this?"
